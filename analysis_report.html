Executive Summary

This pipeline provides an exploratory framework for integrating Ribo-seq with RNA-seq to identify broad translation-associated 3'UTR trends in human glioma/brain tissue. It produces two distinct outputs: (1) a gene-level exploratory TE analysis identifying population-level design rules, and (2) transcript-level provisional 3'UTR candidates with full provenance metadata, design filters, and appropriate caveats.

Key Takeaways

Strongest supported finding: Shorter annotated 3'UTRs are associated with higher apparent TE in this dataset (~1.6-fold across the full length range). This is consistent with established biology and likely generalizable.
Critical caveat: Candidate 3'UTR sequences are exploratory leads from glioma-associated data, not validated design-ready parts. Gene-level TE cannot be attributed to a specific 3'UTR isoform without transcript-level validation. All candidates require experimental testing in the target cell type.

1. Workflow and Methods

This pipeline integrates ribosome profiling (Ribo-seq) with RNA-seq to quantify translational efficiency (TE) across human genes and extract 3'UTR design rules for synthetic mRNA constructs. Eight samples from GEO series GSE61334 (human glioma tumor vs adjacent normal tissue) were processed through the following steps:

1

Adapter Trimming (fastp)

Sequencing adapters and low-quality bases removed. Ribo-seq reads capped at 35 nt (--length_limit 35) to remove non-RPF fragments. Minimum read length: 20 nt.

2

rRNA Depletion (bowtie2)

Reads aligned to NCBI RefSeq human rRNA sequences (5S, 5.8S, 18S, 28S). Non-rRNA reads extracted with --un-gz. Ribo-seq samples showed 50-61% rRNA contamination (typical for ribosome profiling); RNA-seq showed 2-17%.

3

Genome Alignment (STAR 2.7.11b)

Reads mapped to GRCh38 primary assembly with GENCODE v46 annotations. Modality-specific parameters ensure appropriate handling of short RPFs vs spliced mRNA fragments.

Ribo-seq Parameters

  • --alignEndsType EndToEnd no soft-clipping
  • --alignIntronMax 1 no de novo junctions
  • --outFilterMultimapNmax 1 unique only
  • --outFilterMismatchNmax 2

RNA-seq Parameters

  • Default splice-aware mode
  • Annotated + novel junctions
  • Multi-mappers reported (filtered later)
  • --quantMode GeneCounts
4

Gene-Level Counting (featureCounts)

Ribo-seq: -t CDS -s 1 counts only coding sequence regions (sense strand) to avoid 3'UTR read-through circularity.
RNA-seq: -t exon -s 0 counts all exonic regions (unstranded) for total transcript abundance.
Both: -Q 10 --primary (MAPQ ≥ 10, primary alignments only).

5

Transcript-Level Quantification (salmon)

Salmon pseudo-alignment quantifies transcript-level TPM for all 8 samples against the GENCODE v46 transcriptome. This identifies the dominant translated isoform per gene (highest Ribo-seq TPM) and enables transcript-level TE computation, partially addressing the gene-to-isoform attribution problem. Note: salmon on short, single-end RPF reads has intrinsic transcript assignment ambiguity; gene-level TE fallback is used when transcript-level data is unavailable.

6

Normalization (Median-of-Ratios)

DESeq2-style normalization implemented in Python. Geometric means computed over non-zero values (sparse-aware). Ribo-seq and RNA-seq normalized independently.

7

Translational Efficiency (dual-level)

Gene-level: log2(TE) = log2(Ribo + 0.5) - log2(RNA + 0.5) from featureCounts. Used for broad trend analysis.
Transcript-level: Ribo TPM / RNA TPM per isoform from salmon. Used for candidate ranking, attributed to the dominant translated isoform.

8

3'UTR Coverage & Read-Through

Ribo-seq and RNA-seq bigWig tracks averaged across biological replicates using deepTools bigwigAverage. Coverage profiled over genomic spans of 3'UTR regions (BED coordinates; for multi-exonic UTRs this may include intronic space). Sequence export is splice-aware, but coverage profiling approximates the actual spliced 3'UTR body. RTE computed with fixed 12 nt P-site offset (literature default, not empirically calibrated for this dataset): (UTRribo/CDSribo) / (UTRrna/CDSrna).

9

3'UTR Design Scoring & Filtering

Candidates must pass binary PASS/FAIL filters before scoring: polyA signal present, no homopolymer runs >6 nt, RNA secondary structure (MFE via ViennaRNA), miRNA seed-match burden. Passing candidates scored: 40% TE + 25% length + 15% GC + 20% stop context. TE gate (log2 > 0). Each candidate exported with full provenance: transcript ID, isoform rank, exon count, filter results.

Candidate Caveats

  • Gene-to-isoform attribution: Gene-level TE may not reflect the specific 3'UTR isoform selected. Salmon-derived transcript TE (where available) partially addresses this but is not equivalent to direct isoform-level validation.
  • Tissue specificity: Brain/glioma-enriched genes (e.g., KCNH7, CD163) show high TE in this dataset but may not transfer to HEK293, CHO, or other expression systems.
  • Expression bias: TE rankings are inherently biased toward highly expressed genes (50+ read minimum). Top candidates tend to be abundant housekeeping genes, partly due to this selection effect.
  • RTE coverage: Only ~443 of ~11,000 genes had sufficient signal for read-through measurement. The stop_score is informative for only ~4% of candidates.
  • Scoring is heuristic: Weights (40/25/15/20) lack empirical calibration. The ranking is a prioritization tool, not a predictive model.

3. Interpretation

What the Data Tells Us

  1. Shorter 3'UTRs (200-500 nt) are associated with higher TE. This is the strongest supported finding (~2.3-fold across the full length range). It aligns with established biology: shorter UTRs present fewer targets for miRNA-mediated repression and fewer destabilizing elements. Evolutionary selection has shaped housekeeping genes toward shorter UTRs.
  2. Moderate GC content (~35-45%) is weakly favorable. High GC promotes stable secondary structures that can impede ribosome scanning and 43S complex loading. The effect is real but explains only ~1.4% of TE variance, making it a poor predictor for individual sequences.
  3. Top-ranked candidates come from highly expressed, efficiently translated genes (LDHB, HMGCR, HSPA8, CRYAB). These genes have been under evolutionary selection for efficient translation. Tissue-enriched candidates (e.g., KCNH7, CD163) may not transfer to other cell types or growth states.
  4. Low-RTE stop codon contexts can be flagged. Genes with RTE < 1 show apparent efficient termination in this dataset, based on the fixed 12 nt P-site approximation. This supports exploratory flagging of favorable stop contexts but not firm mechanistic claims about termination efficiency.
  5. Read-through is detectable but rare. After filtering artifacts, only a handful of genes show elevated RTE (1.0-3.0). However, distinguishing true read-through from re-initiation or ribosome queuing requires additional evidence beyond coverage ratios.

What the Data Does NOT Tell Us

  1. Causation. All correlations are observational. A 3'UTR from a high-TE gene may confer high TE, or the gene may be high-TE for entirely unrelated reasons (promoter strength, codon usage, 5'UTR structure, protein stability). The 3'UTR sequence alone does not determine TE.
  2. Cell-type specificity. This dataset is from one tissue context (glioma tumor vs adjacent normal brain). The same 3'UTR may behave differently in HEK293, CHO, or primary human cells where the miRNA and RNA-binding protein landscape differs dramatically.
  3. Which specific sequence elements within the 3'UTR drive TE. The analysis computes bulk features (GC%, ARE density, length) but does not identify individual regulatory motifs (specific miRNA binding sites, RNA-binding protein motifs, secondary structures). A 500 nt UTR with optimal bulk features could still contain a potent silencing element.
  4. mRNA stability vs translation rate. TE as measured here (Ribo-seq/RNA-seq ratio) conflates two processes: how efficiently ribosomes load onto an mRNA, and how stable the mRNA is. A gene with rapid mRNA degradation but normal ribosome loading will appear to have high TE.
  5. Whether the design score predicts performance. The scoring weights (40/25/15/20) are heuristic and uncalibrated. Without experimental validation, the ranking is a hypothesis, not a result. The score identifies genes with high TE and short UTRs; it may simply rediscover housekeeping genes rather than identify superior 3'UTR sequences.
  6. Codon context effects. The last ~30 codons before the stop codon influence termination efficiency and read-through propensity, but these are not analyzed. Optimal stop codon context (which nucleotides follow the stop) is not extracted.

4. Limitations

Experimental Design

  • Low Ribo-seq replication: n=1 normal, n=2 tumor Ribo-seq replicates; underpowered for between-condition statistics. All tumor-vs-normal comparisons are purely descriptive with no statistical power for individual gene calls.
  • Unbalanced replication: 3 normal vs 2 tumor RNA-seq replicates complicates per-condition averaging.
  • Sample identity caveat: SRR1562541/SRR1562542 assignment to tumor/normal was inferred from GEO metadata and should be independently verified.

Data Quality

  • RPF length (26.6 nt) is below the expected 28-31 nt, suggesting mild RNase over-digestion. Reduces confidence in codon-resolution analyses.
  • Frame 0 periodicity (41-44%) is below the 45% quality threshold. The data is genuine Ribo-seq (well above the 33% random baseline) but a fraction of reads may be degradation products that survived size selection.
  • Approximate P-site correction. A fixed 12 nt offset (literature default for human RPFs) is applied for RTE computation. This is an approximation; full metagene-calibrated, length-specific offsets would provide sharper boundary resolution but were not computed.

Analytical Limitations

  • Bulk feature correlations are weak. GC (r = -0.08) and ARE density (r = +0.04) explain <1% of TE variance. These are population-level trends, not individual-sequence predictors.
  • Design score is heuristic. Weights (40/25/15/20) lack empirical calibration. The stop_score component is effectively inactive for 96% of genes (neutral score of 0.5), making the practical weighting ~53% TE, ~33% length, ~20% GC.
  • Tissue specificity. This is glioma/brain tissue data. miRNA profiles, RBP expression, and ribosome composition differ across cell types. Tissue-enriched candidates (e.g., KCNH7, CD163) may not perform well in HEK293, CHO, or other expression systems.
  • Expression bias. TE rankings are biased toward highly expressed genes because the method requires 50+ reads in both assays. Top candidates are housekeeping genes partly due to this selection effect.
  • Design filters are basic. PolyA signal, homopolymers, miRNA seed matches, and MFE are now computed, but these are simple scans. Full miRNA target prediction (TargetScan context scores), comprehensive RBP motif analysis, and experimental miRNA/RBP expression profiling in the target cell type would strengthen candidate selection.
  • Gene-to-isoform attribution remains approximate. Salmon transcript-level quantification identifies the dominant isoform, but alternative polyadenylation and tissue-specific isoform usage may cause the observed TE to reflect a different 3'UTR than the one selected.

5. Recommended Next Steps

Priority 1: Cross-Cell-Type Validation

The single biggest limitation of this analysis is tissue specificity. All findings derive from one tissue context (glioma/brain). Running the same pipeline on multiple cell types would separate universal UTR properties from tissue-specific effects and build a consensus ranking with dramatically higher confidence.

The pipeline is already parameterized and portable. Suggested public datasets with matched Ribo-seq + RNA-seq:

Cell TypeRationaleGEO Accession(s)
HEK293Most common mammalian expression host; directly relevant for synthetic construct designGSE94460, GSE73136
HeLaWidely used, extensive published data; enables comparison with many existing studiesGSE58207, GSE65885
K562ENCODE reference cell line; hematopoietic lineage provides tissue diversityENCODE Ribo-seq
MCF7Breast cancer; tests generalizability beyond neural tissueGSE86512
Mouse liver / ESCCross-species validation; tests whether design rules are evolutionarily conservedVarious (mouse GRCm39)

Expected outputs from multi-cell-type analysis:

  • Per-cell-type TE rankings and design scores using identical methodology
  • Consensus ranking: UTRs that score highly across 3+ cell types are the strongest candidates
  • Tissue-specificity coefficient for each UTR (variance in rank across cell types)
  • Validation of design rules: does the length-TE correlation hold universally?
  • Cell-type-specific miRNA and RBP effects revealed by divergent rankings

Priority 2: Before Ordering Synthesis

  1. Full miRNA target prediction (TargetScan 8.0 context scores, miRDB) for the intended host cell line. The pipeline's 7-mer seed scan is a rough filter; context-dependent prediction is more reliable.
  2. Review MFE and structure predictions in the exported CSV. Flag candidates with MFE < -0.3 kcal/mol/nt or predicted stable hairpins near the polyA signal.
  3. Verify polyA signal architecture in the FASTA headers. Candidates marked "none" for polyA signal should be deprioritized or manually inspected.
  4. Include positive controls: HBB (beta-globin), HBA1 (alpha-globin), and AES 3'UTRs as established benchmarks. If candidates don't outperform these, the analysis hasn't added value.

Priority 3: Computational Extensions

  • Full motif enrichment analysis: scan candidates against RBPDB, UTRdb for RNA-binding protein motifs beyond the basic miRNA seed scan.
  • Codon context analysis: examine the last 30-60 codons before the stop codon for optimal usage and termination context.
  • Metagene P-site calibration: empirically determine read-length-specific P-site offsets around start codons for this dataset, replacing the 12 nt default.

Priority 4: Experimental Validation

  • Reporter assay: clone top 5-10 candidate 3'UTRs downstream of Firefly luciferase in the target cell type. This is the critical validation step.
  • Massively parallel reporter assay (MPRA): 100-200 candidates with truncation variants to identify minimal functional elements.
  • Synthetic 3'UTR design: combine rules (200-400 nt, 38-42% GC, no predicted miRNA sites, minimal structure) into fully synthetic constructs and benchmark against natural candidates.
